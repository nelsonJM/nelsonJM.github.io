You have an idea for an app that you think a small number[1] of eager people will hire to do a particular job for them.

  • Instead of building it right away, make sure you know what job the product really does for your customers[2] and you can summarize the job. Interviewing[3] customers/potential customers is a huge part of this step and will yield a good deal of info.
    • you'll learn about their past and current behavior,
    • other products they've hired,
    • the motivations and anxieties (forces[4])surrounding the purchase of those other products
  • You should now have the job-what the solution you plan to build should accomplish. You can use it to:
    • guide marketing efforts
    • guide design and development efforts

Knowing the job your product is hired to do helps you prioritize features and make tradeoffs[5] in product design

Your features are smaller jobs that support the main job and can be summarized in the form of a "job story"[6] (situation + motivation and forces = outcome)

  • each feature is composed of a broad job story, flows, screens, domain-specific language and a domain model
  • each screen of your app can have several job stories that all support the larger feature job story
  • the UI elements, domain language, and models that are on each screen must be arranged in a way that resolves the motivations/forces and anxieties[4][7] that are tied to each job on the screen
