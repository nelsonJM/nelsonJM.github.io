Every 4 weeks I have the responsibility of counting 3 groups of people and sharing those numbers with others. I decided to design a Counter app to help me count as well as keep track of counts.

In Part I, I'll talk about the research process of the project and in Part II I'll cover the idea generation, prototyping, and final design.

Before I begin:

  1. I am not an expert practitioner of the Jobs to Be Done approach to customer research and product design. I appreciate the importance that Jobs to Be Done places on understanding what customer's are trying to accomplish in a given context and the tradeoffs they are willing to make to achieve their goals.
  2. I'm designing this for myself. There's a chance I may interview others, but for now I will be the primary research target.

Part I: Research and Identifying the
Job-to-be-Done

I'l be using the Job Outline to help in researching the problem and determining what the solution should accomplish. The goal of the outline is to identify the job that customers want done. Here are the four parts of the job outline:

  1. Exposition
  2. Observation
  3. Situational Analysis
  4. Job-to-be-Done

I use job stories as a tool for summarizing the job and capturing key constraints, motivations and forces. The job story for the Counter app is:

Job Story

"When it's my turn to count the number of attendees in the meeting and I may be interrupted at any moment by an attendee, I want to be able to count and keep track of the current count at the same time, so that I have an accurate count to send to the recordkeeper(s)."

Exposition - The Context of the Customer's Struggle

Understanding the context helps me understand the problem I am solving.

Each month, myself and a helper have the responsibility to count the number of people who meet together in an elementary school. This group of people can be divided into the following subgroups: attendees, childcare workers, and children. My helper and I split the counting so that one of us will only count attendees and the other will only count childcare workers and the children. After the meeting we combine our counts and one of us will send the totals to a designated recordkeeper who oversees meeting attendance as well as other details.

Observation

Observing how the problem has been solved in the past and how it is currently being solved provides me with real behavior I can "interrogate" and analyze.

My goal is to observe how I currently solve the problem, discover the products I have used in the past and that I'm currently using, and learn more about the context of the struggle.

Since I'm designing this app for myself, I conducted my observation using a combination of recall and "on-the-job" notetaking, meaning I recorded some of my actions, thoughts and observations while performing the counting task.

Now is a good time to mention that counting is only one of my responsibilities. I have listed my additional responsibilities below:

  • Helper and I need to arrive at the elementary school by 9:45 a.m and set up chairs for the attendees in the cafeteria.
  • Set up signs that indicate where the restrooms and the nursing mothers room are located.
  • Place the children's crafts and supplies in the designated classroom.
  • Greet people as they arrive.
  • Pass out meeting material to those who need it.
  • Help attendees find seating as they arrive
  • Count the attendees, childcare workers and children.
  • Text count to record-keeper
  • Pass out announcements at close of meeting
  • "Break down" chair set up and place chairs on designated carts
  • Put away all signs
  • Leave meeting areas in order we found them.

My Observations - What, Where, When, and Why

Below are some of my observations of my behavior before, during, and after performing the activity of counting attendees. I decided to first present the observed behavior (the 'What', 'Where', 'When') followed by questions (the yellow box) related to the observation, that I asked myself in order to determine the 'why' or causality behind my behavior as well as my answers.

Arrive at the School

I arrive at the school around 9:45 a.m., park in the school lot, enter the school and head straight to the cafeteria where the main meeting will take place.

Chair Setup

There are 3-4 carts filled with stacked or folded chairs that are waiting either in the cafeteria or the hallway. If necessary, my helper and I move the carts of chairs from the hallway into the cafeteria. We unload the chairs and form rows arranged in a polygon. The first row is shortest, 4 chairs, then the next is 7 chairs, then 10, and so on. When all the chairs are arranged and straightened, we push the carts out into the hallway.

Setup Signs and Supplies

Next, I or the helper, go to the janitor's office and get the restroom signs, nursing moms sign and children's supplies. Each item has a specific part of the building it belongs.

Attendees Arrive

Around 10:00am, people begin to trickle in. Some put things on the chairs but most stand near the complementary food and drink coffee. Around 10:30am, the meeting begins and people walk to the chairs and begin looking for a seat.

During the Meeting

As the meeting progresses, some more people arrive late and sit on fold out tables if a chair is not open. Some leave their seat and return later.

Counting

Around 11:00 a.m., I create a new note using the Notability or Simplenote app, stand up, lean against the wall behind me and begin counting attendees who are sitting in chairs. I do this during a time when people are focused on someone at the opposite side of the room. I'll move around a little, but not too much because i don't want any attention. I don't move my arms or hands much, just my lips as I count. When I have the number, I record it in my open note. Next, I'll count the people who chose to sit at the folding tables or elsewhere and add them to the numbers on my note. Meanwhile, my helper has left the cafeteria to count the number of childcare workers and children.

Understanding the 'Why' behind the behavior

Why do I stand up when the audience is not focused on my general direction? So that I am less noticeable. I don't like to draw attention to myself.

Do I do anything else while I'm counting? I hold my phone, put one hand in my pocket, or try in some way to look casual (or comfortable?) and try to focus on counting.

How do I get a better look when you have trouble seeing everyone? I walk towards the opposite corner or along the side of the room. I've also tried counting the number of empty chairs (since I know the number we initially set up). This helps speed up the counting.

Does that method of counting work for me? As long as the meeting is not too big, yes. If it is a large meeting (which is common), and we are periodically adding chairs or entire rows to accomodate people, then it is tough to keep track of the number of additional chairs.

What does the counting process look like when I count childcare workers and children? The workers and children are divided into 3 groups: toddlers, preschoolers, and older kids. The preschoolers and older kids meet in two classrooms. I will walk to each classroom, peek in, and count both the children and the workers. I do this in my head and as I am walking to the next group of people to count, I input the numbers in my Notability or Simplenote app.

What do I input - just numbers and a quick note? I type "workers", then the number of workers and "children", then the number of children. Sometimes I'll just type the numbers stacked with the number of workers on top and the children below since there are usually more children than workers and it's easy to distinguish which number corresponds to which group.

Which tools have I used in the past to record the counts? I've used Evernote, Simplenote, Notability, Apple's notes app, my memory, and pen and paper.

Is there one that I use more often? I move between Simplenote and Notability. Simplenote feels easier to open a new note and have it ready. Notability and especially Evernote have more UI chrome and feel like overload for a simple task. By 'overload', I mean that if I had scratch paper and a pencil, that's all I'd need, but I would look like I'm working too much. I want the paper and pencil simplicity but on the phone so I don't look like I'm working hard.

Why don't I want to look like I'm working hard? I want to do a good job but I also want the right perspective and appropriate behavior. The meeting is comfortable, familiar and has a somewhat casual atmosphere. I don't want to stick out like a rigid, "by the books" official taking a census.

So when it's time to count, I stand up, off to the side, open Simplenote and begin counting - do I input as I count or count first? I open Simplenote after I count or before. If I'm counting attendees in the main meeting, I usually count first, hold the number in memory, sit down or walk out, take out my phone and input the counts in Simplenote along with a label like "meeting". Something I noticed while counting children was that I cannot count and input the numbers at the same time, but I really wish I could. Instead, I have to count, open Simplenote or at least unlock my phone, tap the note to bring up the keyboard, type the number and sometimes the label, too. That seems like a lot of work.

Sending the Counts

When my helper returns, we exchange counts and one of us (usually the one who combines the counts) will send all three counts (attendees, childcare workers, and children) to someone who keeps track of meeting attendance.

Understanding the 'Why' behind the behavior

Who sends the count and who receives the count? It goes to one person who I'm calling the 'recordkeeper'. I'm sure what that person does with it. In the past, we would write down the count on a piece of paper and place it in a folder. It's not a rule, but the person who counts the attendees in the meeting is usually the one who compiles the numbers and sends them to the recordkeeper.

What's my process for sending the count? I send it as a text message. If I'm the one who counted the attendees, then I'll get the numbers from my helper and add them to my Simplenote 'note', like this:

Meeting: 100

Workers: 3

Children: 10

Then I'll 'long press' the note to bring up the context menu, press 'Select all', press 'Copy', then switch over to the messages app, create a new message addressed to the recordkeeper, type something like 'Here's today's meeting count', paste the count into the message and send.

Situational Analysis

My goal is to design with forces in mind. There are forces within the environment and situation surrounding my product that will impact the final design of the solution. There are also motivations within the customer that must be reconciled by my design. My aim is to balance or reconcile those forces and motivations using my domain knowledge and design patterns.

The job outline makes use of the forces diagram to perform the situational analysis. I like how the diagram breaks up the "forces" and motivations into 4 distinct areas, listed below:

  • Push of the situation
  • Magnetism of the new solution
  • Habit of the present
  • Anxiety of the new solution

Here is that same list, only this time I added some of the forces and motivations that I discovered through my observations and questioning:

  • Push of the situation:
    • I want fewer steps to get the job done
    • Too much typing is a pain
    • Exchanging counts while the meeting is still going on is disruptive.
    • I want to count and record my count at the same time, without having to switch my eyes back and forth from phone to people
    • I don't want to look like I'm doing a lot of work
  • Magnetism of the new solution:
    • Simple and fast like using Simplenote
    • Remembers my counts so I don't have to worry about being interrupted while counting and having to start over
    • Easy to send counts to others. Fewer steps
    • Keeps track of previous month's counts
  • Habit of the present:
    • Simplenote is easy, like pen and paper - what if the new solution is too complicated? I like the simple, blank note that reminds me of scratch paper
  • Anxiety of the new solution:
    • Counting and recording at the same time is nice, but I'm worried I'll make a mistake since I won't be looking at my phone as often
    • The new solution seems to place importance on tracking past counts. That sounds like more stuff to learn. I'm not sure I need that capability

The Job-to-Be-Done

At the start of this post, I mentioned what I believe is the Job-to-Be-Done by this counter app. Here it is again:

"When it's my turn to count the number of attendees in the meeting and I may be interrupted at any moment by an attendee, I want to be able to count and keep track of the current count at the same time, so that I have an accurate count to send to the recordkeeper(s)."

I like the way the Job-to-be-Done is defined in the job outline article (here's the link again, http://jobstobedone.org/news/filling-in-leans-gaps/):

'Job' definition from 'Filling in Lean's Gaps'

"The Job to be Done is not a solution, but a way of thinking about what the solution should accomplish. It’s not a shot in the dark hypothesis, but rather the result of deep thinking and a coalescing of research."
- Alan Klement

Understanding what the solution should accomplish (the Job) is a prerequisite to deciding the form that the solution will take. In Part II, I'll begin to explore the possible forms through idea generation, sketching, prototyping, and final design.
